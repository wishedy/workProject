Synopsis
8 ACE Reports (7 randomized controlled trials and 1 systematic review and meta-analysis, n=694) from the OrthoEvidence database assessing the effectiveness of corticosteroid injections in comparison to control injections (either saline or local anesthesia) for the treatment of lateral epicondylitis were included in this review. The purpose of this review was to determine the impact of corticosteroid injections on pain and physical function in comparison to these control treatments. As such, information and data on platelet-rich plasma (PRP) injection efficacy as a treatment was not included from reports. Statistical pooling of VAS pain scores and Quick DASH scores exhibited that corticosteroid injections provided significant pain relief, compared to control injections only in the short-term (6-12 weeks), while having no functional benefits over control injections at any time point within a 6 month period. Similar results were reflected in PRTEE pain and functional disability scores for the treatment of lateral epicondylitis with corticosteroid injections versus control injections.

Why was this study needed now?
Lateral epicondylitis, commonly referred to as tennis elbow, is a painful elbow condition generated from work or non-work related overuse and involves the wear of the extensor tendon attached to the lateral epicondyle. Conservative treatment modalities for lateral epicondylitis range from physiotherapy to a number of injectable interventions, a common treatment being corticosteroid injections. However, conflicting evidence exists regarding the efficacy of the use of corticosteroid injections in the treatment of this disorder. Hence, this ACE review aimed to compare of corticosteroid injections against control injections (saline or local anesthesia) on pain and function in patients with lateral epicondylitis.
What was the principal research question?
Is the use of corticosteroid injections more effective than a control treatment (either saline or local anesthesia) for the improvement of pain and functional outcomes in patients with lateral epicondylitis measured up to 12 months?

What were the important findings?
- 7 randomized controlled trials (n=384) and 1 systematic review and network meta-analysis (n=310) comparing the effects of corticosteroid versus a control injection (either saline or local anaesthetic) on pain in patients with lateral epicondylitis were included in this review. - VAS pain was reported in a total of 5 studies (n=298). At 2-4 weeks (5 studies, n=298), there were no significant differences in VAS pain between groups (MD = -1.61; 95% CI -3.47 to 0.25), however, there was a trend favouring the corticosteroid group (p=0.09; I-squared = 92%). At 6-12 weeks (3 studies, n=177), VAS pain improved significantly more in the corticosteroid group (MD = -1.50; 95% CI -2.23 to -0.77; p<0.0001; I-squared = 0%), however, there was no significant difference between groups at 6 months (4 studies, n=184) (MD = 0.28; 95% CI -0.51 to 1.07; p=0.49; I-squared = 69%). - The overall treatment effect for corticosteroid injections on VAS pain was not significantly different compared to that of the control injections (MD = -0.83; 95% CI -1.73 to 0.06; p=0.07; I-squared=90%). Similar results were obtained in a recent systematic review and network meta-analysis by Krogh et al. (June 2013), which included 10 trials (n=310; Range of follow-up: 24-52 weeks) and found no significant difference in the treatment effects of corticosteroid injections and placebo injections (SMD: -0.04; 95% CI: -0.45 to 0.35; p=0.81). Four of the studies used in this review were also used in the meta-analysis included (Krogh et al. 2013). - DASH scores were reported in a total of 3 studies (n=196). There was no significance between groups in Quick DASH scores at 2-4 weeks (3 studies, n=196) (MD = -11.58; 95% CI -32.99 to 9.83; p=0.29; I-squared = 90%), at 2-3 months (2 studies, n=118) (MD = -2.28; 95% CI -17.95 to 13.39; p=0.78; I-squared = 56%), or at 6 months (2 studies, n=75) (MD = 4.08; 95% CI -3.38 to 11.53; p=0.28; I-squared = 0%). - The pain and function subscales of the PRTEE were reported in 2 studies (n=58). Krogh et al. (March 2013) (n=40) found that there were initial significant improvements in pain and function in favour of the corticosteroid group at 1 month (Pain: p=0.011; Function: p=0.003), but this significance was lost at 3 months (Pain: p=0.229; Function: p=0.310). Wolf et al. (n=18) reported that both the corticosteroid and control groups improved significantly from baseline to 2 weeks (Pain: p=0.033) and to 6 months (Pain: p<0.01; Function: p<0.01). However, there were no significant differences between the two groups at 2 weeks, 2 months and 6 months (p=0.147). - 3 studies reported grip strength data (n=162). Price et al. found a significant improvement in grip strength (mmHg) at 4 weeks in favour of the corticosteroid group; however this was lost at the 8- and 24-week follow-ups. An additional 2 studies (Lindenhovius et al. and Newcomer et al.) found no significant difference in grip strength (kg) between the corticosteroid and control groups at any time point (4 weeks, 8 weeks, and 6 months) (p>0.05). - One study (Mardani-Kivi et al.) examined recurrence rates, defined as an increased VAS score to 50% at 12 weeks, compared with 3- or 6-week visits. They found a 34.7% recurrence rate in the corticosteroid group and a 0% recurrence rate in the control group. The relevance of this finding, however, is uncertain considering there was a lack of improvement in VAS pain scores observed in the control group at 3 and 6 weeks, indicating an inability for the condition to recur in patients.
What should I remember most?
Treatment of lateral epicondylitis with corticosteroid injections provided an early positive effect on pain; however this trend was lost at 6 months, where no significant difference was found between corticosteroid injections and control injections. Overall though, the treatment effect for corticosteroid injections in the management of lateral epicondylitis was found to be not significantly different than that of control injections. This finding was mirrored in a recent meta-analysis (Krogh et al., 2013), which also found no significant difference in the overall treatment effects of corticosteroid and control injections. In addition, corticosteroid injections had no significant effect on function in these patients.
How will this affect the care of patients?
The results from this review indicate that, although beneficial in the short term, corticosteroid injections may not be useful for long-term treatment of pain caused by lateral epicondylitis. Furthermore, corticosteroid injections do not seem to provide any short- or long-term benefits in function and disability. It is important to note that this review found no significant detrimental effects of corticosteroid injections, however the 1 year time period may not have been long enough to detect these changes. Future studies should aim to investigate whether corticosteroid injections yield higher recurrence rates compared to control injections over a longer follow-up period. Alternative long-term treatments for lateral epicondylitis, such as physiotherapy, should be another focus of future research.
